## SECTION 3: CUSTOMER SEGMENTS AND JOBS

### Customer Mix

**Fact:** WD-40 serves three primary customer bands:

| Segment | Estimated Revenue Share | Channel | Pain Points Addressed |
|---------|------------------------|---------|----------------------|
| Consumer / DIY (retail) | ~50–55% | Hardware, mass retail, e-commerce | Rust, corrosion, stuck parts, squeaks |
| Professional / Trade (MRO, automotive, industrial) | ~35–40% | Wholesale, MRO distributors, B2B | Maintenance efficiency, asset preservation |
| Institutional / OEM | ~5–10% | Direct, specialty distributors | Production line lubrication, specification compliance |

**Analysis: Mission-Criticality and Switching Costs**

- WD-40 MUP is a **low-cost, high-criticality consumable** in MRO workflows. A can of WD-40 retails for ~$6–$10 (US); the cost of a seized bolt or rusted mechanism in an industrial setting may be 100–1,000× that value. This creates extremely high implicit switching costs relative to product cost.
- **Do-nothing prevalence:** Low in professional/industrial (failure cost is high); moderate in consumer (alternatives include competing sprays, household oils). The consumer who "does nothing" simply lives with a squeaky hinge—low revenue impact per incident.
- **Procurement blocker:** In institutional/OEM, specification approval cycles (6–18 months) create both a barrier to entry and a barrier to exit. Once WD-40 is on an approved supplier list, displacement requires a formal de-specification process.
- **Switching-cost quantification:** Conservatively estimated at 5–10× product cost in professional/industrial (labor cost to re-evaluate, qualify, and re-train outweighs any pricing concession a competitor could offer). In consumer, switching cost is near zero—brand loyalty is the primary retention mechanism.

**Inference:** The consumer segment's loyalty is sentiment/habit-based, not structural. A sustained quality issue or aggressive private-label pricing (e.g., retailer own-brand at 30–40% discount) could disrupt consumer repeat rates. This is the segment most vulnerable in an economic downturn.

## SECTION 10: PRICING POWER AND ELASTICITY TESTING

### Pricing Governance

**Fact:** WD-40 Company does not disclose a formal pricing council or governance document. Pricing decisions are made regionally by segment presidents with corporate oversight. Evidence of pricing discipline:

- **FY2022:** Implemented ~8–12% price increases across most markets in response to commodity cost inflation. Volume impact was modest (estimated –2 to –4% volume offset based on revenue vs. volume analysis), implying **price elasticity of demand (PED) of approximately –0.25 to –0.40** (relatively inelastic).
- **FY2023–FY2024:** More modest ~3–5% increases as commodity pressure eased; volume recovered.

### Elasticity Evidence

**Analysis:**

| Price Action | Time Period | Price Increase | Volume Impact | Implied PED |
|-------------|------------|---------------|---------------|-------------|
| Commodity-driven increase | FY2022 | +8–12% | –2 to –4% | –0.25 to –0.40 |
| Normalized increase | FY2023–24 | +3–5% | ~flat to +1% | ~0.0 to –0.25 |

**Inference:** PED of –0.25 to –0.40 is consistent with a **strongly inelastic demand product**—a hallmark of genuine pricing power. Comparable consumer staples with similar PED include Scotch tape (3M), Sharpie (Newell Brands), and certain OTC pharmaceutical categories.

### ARPU Ceiling Estimate

**Analysis:** Current average revenue per unit (US retail ~$7–10/can). Private-label alternatives retail at ~$4–6. The **premium ceiling** before material consumer switching is estimated at ~$12–14/can (implying ~40–70% premium to private label), versus current ~$7–10 (implying ~30–50% premium). This suggests **2–3 more years of modest price increases** before reaching PED inflection.

## SECTION 17: SUPPLY CHAIN AND OPERATIONS

### Critical Supply Chain Inputs

**Fact/Analysis:**

| Input | Supplier Concentration | Risk Level | Alternative Sourcing |
|-------|----------------------|------------|---------------------|
| Petroleum distillates (base oil) | Multiple suppliers; global commodity | Medium | Substitutable; price risk > supply risk |
| Aerosol propellants (LPG, dimethyl ether) | 2–4 key suppliers per region | Medium | Moderately substitutable |
| Aerosol cans (tinplate) | Regional manufacturers; 2–3 key suppliers | Medium-High | Lead time 3–6 months to qualify alternate |
| Plastic spray nozzles/straw components | Multiple Asian manufacturers | Low-Medium | Substitutable; modest lead time |
| Contract manufacturers (blending) | Mix of owned production (US, UK) and contract manufacturers (APAC, EMEA) | Medium | Geographic diversification exists |

**Single Point of Failure:** **Aerosol can supply**. Tinplate steel availability and can-manufacturer capacity concentration (3–4 major suppliers globally) creates a moderate supply risk. During the 2021–2022 supply chain crisis, aerosol can shortages impacted multiple consumer goods companies. WD-40 navigated this but reported cost headwinds.

**Manufacturing Economics:**
- Gross margin improvement trajectory is partially supply-chain-driven: as petroleum distillate costs normalize and aerosol component costs stabilize, blended Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) per unit should decline 3–5% in real terms FY2025–2027.
- **COGS sensitivity to crude oil price:** A $10/barrel change in Brent crude approximately affects WD-40's petroleum-derivative input costs by ~$3–5M (~0.5–0.9% of revenue), or ~50–90 bps of gross margin. This is material but manageable.
